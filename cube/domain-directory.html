Data & Analytics · Domain Directory

A simple guide to Pelago's data domains

Each domain is a focused slice of Pelago data. This page walks through what each one tracks, in plain language, with a real-world example so you can pick the right one.

How to read this page

Every domain card below follows the same three-step pattern. Read it once here, and the rest of the page will feel familiar.

The pattern

1
The building block. The smallest piece of information the domain tracks. Think of it like a single Lego brick. Every other view in the domain is built by stacking these together.
2
How it adds up. When you stack many building blocks together, what bigger picture can you see? Trends, totals, populations, journeys.
3
What it connects to. The other context you can layer alongside the building blocks. This is how a single fact becomes a richer story (e.g. "this appointment, for this member, working on this diagnosis, paid for by this client").
A note on the examples. To make things concrete, the examples follow a fictional member named Jane, an employee at a fictional client called Acme Corp. Wherever you see Jane, picture a real Pelago member moving through her care.
The bigger picture

Why we organize data this way

Why does Pelago slice its data into domains in the first place? As we build toward an AI agent that can answer questions across the whole company, that agent needs structure to draw on. Domains are that structure.

Two pulls, in opposite directions

Why specialists

Specialists give better answers.

An agent focused on a single domain has the right schema, the right prompts, the right retrieval. Higher accuracy, lower hallucination, easier to evaluate. The catch: a specialist alone can't answer something cross-functional, like "what's driving member churn across product, support, and billing?"

Why one front door

People want one place to ask.

If users have to pick the right agent before they even ask a question, most won't. They'll bail. Cognitive load and wrong routing kill adoption. People need a single conversational interface, not a directory of bots to choose from.

How we resolve it: frontstage and backstage

We split the work in two. What the user sees, and what runs behind it.

Frontstage · what the user sees
One enterprise agent

Interprets the question. Decides which domains are relevant. Calls one specialist or several. Synthesizes a single, clean answer.

↓
Backstage · how it gets the answer
Sales Eligibility Member Journey Engagement Appointments Outcomes + more

Domain specialists, one per slice of Pelago data. Each has its own retrieval tuned to that data, domain-specific prompts and tools, and returns structured outputs the orchestrator can compose.

Mental model: the user talks to the CEO. The CEO delegates to department heads. Department heads use their teams and tools to dig in. The CEO comes back with the synthesized answer.

When each layer comes into play

Deep analysis inside one domain One specialist
Cross-functional question Orchestrator pulls from many
Simple, direct question Orchestrator handles it
Power user (analyst) Can hit a specialist directly

The win condition is simple. Users get expert-level answers without ever having to know any of the architecture underneath.

Frontstage: one agent · Backstage: many

What this lets us avoid

  • One giant agent loaded with every dataset, which gets noisy and brittle as the data grows
  • Too many visible agents, which becomes a UX nightmare and quietly tanks adoption
  • No orchestration layer, which leads to inconsistent answers and no clear owner of the response
  • Unstructured outputs, which make cross-domain answers nearly impossible to compose cleanly

The directory below is the menu of those domain specialists. Each card describes one slice of Pelago data: the building block it tracks, how those blocks add up, and what other context they connect to.

01

Reach & Pipeline

Who Pelago could serve, and who we're selling to

Sales Pipeline

Active

Prospective clients we're selling to, before they sign.

1
The building block
One sales opportunity (a single deal Pelago is pursuing). "Acme Corp — annual contract negotiation, currently in proposal stage, opened 42 days ago."
2
How it adds up
Stack opportunities together to see the whole sales funnel: how many deals are at each stage, how long they linger, and how that's trending.
3
What it connects to
  • The parent Account the opportunity is being pursued under (account_id, client_key)
  • The opportunity itself (opportunity_id, distinct count of opportunities)
That's the entire join. Lead source, full client profile, campaigns, and outreach history aren't pulled into this domain.
You can answer things like
  • How long does it take us to close a deal?
  • Where do opportunities tend to get stuck?
  • Which lead sources actually turn into closed business?
Used most by: Sales

Eligibility

Active

Everyone who's covered by a Pelago client and could become a member.

1
The building block
One eligible person. "Jane, an Acme Corp employee, eligible for Pelago's Alcohol and Tobacco programs since March 2024."
2
How it adds up
Stack eligible people together to see whole covered populations: a single client's book, all eligible lives across Pelago, or eligibility broken out by program, geography, or demographics.
3
What it connects to
  • Client profile: name, sub-client, region, industry, account, programs offered, region-lock flag
  • From Member Journey: just member_id (so you can see which eligible people became members)
  • From journey status: just two markers — onboarded_count and care_initiated_count, for funnel math
All the rich eligibility detail (subscriber id, group id, cost centers, health plan info, file metadata) lives directly inside this domain. The joins above are just for cross-referencing.
You can answer things like
  • How many eligible lives do we have this month, by client or program?
  • How long has the average eligible life been covered?
  • What share of eligible lives qualify for more than one program?
Used most by: Implementation team

Growth

Active

Marketing outreach and the path from "never heard of Pelago" to registered member.

1
The building block
One marketing touch (an email send, an open, a click, a landing-page visit). "Jane received the 'Curious about your drinking?' email on April 2, opened it, then clicked through to the landing page."
2
How it adds up
Stack touches together to see a full campaign's performance, an individual's pre-registration journey, or how channels compare against each other.
3
What it connects to
  • The campaign itself (id, name, year, group, status, type, substance, incentive)
  • Campaign experiment metadata (created_date)
  • The full Eligibility profile of the recipient (subscriber, group, programs, file metadata)
  • From Member Journey: basics only — member_id, program, primary_program, is_minor
  • Client profile (name, region, industry, programs offered)
No journey status is joined. You can see if a marketing recipient is a member, but not where they are in the journey, from this domain alone.
You can answer things like
  • How is each campaign performing on opens, clicks, and registrations?
  • Which audiences (program, client, geography) are most likely to engage?
  • Do members who engaged with marketing go on to better outcomes?
Used most by: Growth and acquisition teams
02

Members & Journey

Who became a member, and what they're doing

Member Journey

Active

The "who is this member" foundation. Identity, status, motivations, stage of change.

1
The building block
One registered Pelago member. "Jane, registered May 5, in the Alcohol program, currently in the 'Action' stage of change, motivated by sleeping better."
2
How it adds up
Stack members together to see counts and breakdowns by program, client, status, motivation, or stage of change. This is also the foundation other domains lean on for member-level slicing.
3
What it connects to
  • Eligibility brand and relationship
  • Client profile, plus Youturn reporting (employees / family enrolled)
  • The full journey status: registered, onboarded, enrolled, care-initiated, plus their dates and conversion rates
  • Stage of change (initial & recent), treatment phase, milestone level 1, Zanzibar phase tiers
  • Content steps completed, active messaging streaks, pelatokens earned, HDHP consent
  • Care team interaction counts and averages
  • Primary assigned care team member (full_name, provider_type)
  • Incentives: qualified flag and send status
  • Personalization quiz results
This is the only domain that pulls journey status in full. Everywhere else you'll see just a slice of these fields.
Use this when you need
  • Counts of members by program, client, or status
  • Member-level context (motivation, stage, NPS) to combine with another domain
Used most by: Cross-functional

Engagement

Active

What members do inside the Pelago app and website.

1
The building block
One in-product action (a tap, a feature opened, a page viewed). "Jane opened the 'Cravings' lesson at 8:42pm on April 12 from the iOS app."
2
How it adds up
Stack actions together to see a session, a member's daily or weekly engagement pattern, or feature-level usage across the entire population.
3
What it connects to
  • From Member Journey: basics only — id, name, program, primary_program, age tier, gender, eligibility status, is_minor
  • Just two journey markers: registered_date and care_initiated_date. Nothing else from journey status.
  • Care team interaction counts (totals and per-member averages)
  • Just a count from Member Check-Ins (check_in_count) and a count from Appointments (appointments_attended_count)
  • Outcome highlights: tobacco 4-week quit rate, alcohol clinically-meaningful-change metrics
  • Primary assigned care team member
  • Client profile
Engagement is intentionally a thin tracker of activity, not a full member portrait. For deeper journey or outcomes context, jump to those domains.
You can answer things like
  • How many engagement events happened this week, and which features were most used?
  • Which member segments engage the most?
  • Do more-engaged members get better outcomes?
Used most by: Product teams

Member Check-Ins

Active

What members report about their own progress, week to week.

1
The building block
One self-reported check-in for one program. "Jane's alcohol check-in on April 14: 2 drinks yesterday, mood 'okay,' no urges this morning."
2
How it adds up
Stack check-ins together to see a member's streak, trends over weeks, or program-wide check-in patterns and drop-off.
3
What it connects to
  • From Member Journey: basics only — id, program, primary_program, age tier, gender, eligibility status, is_minor
  • Substance-specific check-in fields: drinks (alcohol), cannabis units, opioid abstinence test, tobacco quantity per day
Notably no client profile and no journey status are joined here. To slice check-ins by client or by where someone is in their journey, you'd combine with Member Journey.
You can answer things like
  • How often do members check in during their first month vs. later?
  • What share of check-ins show a decrease in drinks?
  • How long does it take to show first improvement?
  • When in the journey do check-ins start dropping off?
Used most by: Clinical, Outcomes, Product

Member Orders

Active

The physical stuff Pelago sends to members. NRT, prescriptions, devices.

1
The building block
One ordered item (a single prescription, patch, or device). "A nicotine patch refill shipped to Jane on June 3, delivered June 6."
2
How it adds up
Stack items together to see a full order (often multiple items shipped together), a member's order history, and fulfillment metrics across the book.
3
What it connects to
  • From Member Journey: basics only — id, name, program, primary_program, age tier, gender, eligibility status, is_minor
  • Order detail: item, channel, dates (order_date, status_date), order status, type, origin, price, quantity, fulfillment cost, ShipBob id
  • Client profile (name, region, industry, programs offered)
No journey status is joined. The "vendor" is captured by channel and order_origin on the order itself, not as a separate join.
You can answer things like
  • How many orders went out this month, by program or vendor?
  • What's the average time from order to fulfillment?
  • Which members have orders that are stuck or delayed?
  • What share of orders are repeats or replacements?
Used most by: Clinical team
03

Care Delivery

The clinical work

Appointments

Active

Visits between members and the care team.

1
The building block
One appointment. "Jane's 30-minute video session with Dr. Smith, scheduled for June 10 at 4pm, completed."
2
How it adds up
Stack appointments together to see a member's visit history (1st, 2nd, 3rd appointment), provider caseloads, attendance trends, and time-to-care metrics.
3
What it connects to
  • From Member Journey: basics only — id, program, primary_program, age tier, gender, eligibility status, care_pathway
  • Just four journey markers: is_care_initiated, is_onboarded, registered_date, onboarded_date
  • Just one field from Diagnosis: diagnosis_severity_for_program
  • Eligibility brand and relationship
  • Member-level appointment summary (joined alongside the appointment): first/second/fourth attended-appt rates, conversion rates, days between visits, current-year totals, averages
  • Primary assigned care team member (this is the member's primary provider, not the provider on the specific appt)
  • Client profile
The careful split here: Appointments doesn't pull the full Member Journey or full Diagnosis. It pulls just the few fields the appointment context needs.
You can answer things like
  • How many appointments are scheduled, and how is that changing over time?
  • How long does it take a new member to get to their first visit?
  • How does attendance vary across programs?
  • Are members more likely to no-show their second or third appointment?
Used most by: Clinical Ops, Care Teams, Analytics

Appointment Availability

Active

When members could get seen. Provider supply, before any booking happens.

1
The building block
One open slot on a provider's schedule. "Dr. Smith has an opening Tuesday at 2pm, available to members in California."
2
How it adds up
Stack open slots together to see a provider's full schedule, total system capacity, or access metrics by state or program.
3
What it connects to
  • Just provider name and the schedule slot itself (schedule_key, schedule_count)
Very lean. No member, no client, no program. Pure provider supply.
You can answer things like
  • How much availability do we have, by state and program?
  • How long would a new member typically wait for an opening?
  • Do we have enough capacity in a given region?
Used most by: Clinical Ops, Provider Operations

Care Team Forms

Active

The structured notes providers fill out about members.

1
The building block
One answer to one question on one completed form. On Dr. Smith's intake form for Jane (June 10), one of the entries is "AUDIT score = 14."
2
How it adds up
Stack answers together to see a fully completed form, then a member's clinical record over time, then completion rates by provider or program.
3
What it connects to
  • The form itself (form key, completion count)
  • The provider who filled it out, by id only (no name or type carried in)
  • Member basics from Member Journey (id, name, program, age, gender, state)
  • Client profile (client name, sub-client, region)
No journey status pulled in. If you need to know whether the member was onboarded or in care when the form was filled out, you'd combine this with Member Journey separately.
You can answer things like
  • What share of forms are getting completed?
  • What are providers actually capturing in specific fields?
Used most by: Clinical Ops, Research, Data Science

Diagnosis

Active

Clinical diagnoses over time. Kept separate so it doesn't crowd everyday reporting.

1
The building block
One diagnosis event for one member. "Jane was diagnosed with Alcohol Use Disorder, moderate severity, on May 4."
2
How it adds up
Stack diagnoses together to see a member's full clinical history (severity moving over time) and patterns across the population.
3
What it connects to
  • Member basics from Member Journey (id, name, program, age, gender, state)
  • Client profile (client name, sub-client, region)
  • The full set of AUD/OUD diagnostic criteria fields lives inside the diagnosis record itself
Notably not joined to Appointments or Care Team Forms. The diagnosis stands on its own. If you need to know which appointment or form a diagnosis came from, that's a separate analysis.
You can answer things like
  • How does a member's diagnosis evolve over time?
  • What patterns show up across diagnosis types and severities?
Used most by: Clinical Ops, Research, Advanced Analytics

Program Outcomes

Active

Did the program work? Per-member results for each substance program.

1
The building block
One member's outcome in one program. "Jane's Alcohol outcome: 14 drinks/week at registration → 4 drinks/week at 90 days. Marked as 'reduced-risk drinking.'"
2
How it adds up
Stack member outcomes together to see program-level success rates, outcomes broken out by client, and how member segments compare.
3
What it connects to
  • Member basics from Member Journey (id, name, program, age, gender, state)
  • Six journey markers: registered, onboarded, and care-initiated (counts and dates)
  • One field from Diagnosis: severity for the member's program
  • Per-substance summaries (alcohol, tobacco, opioid, cannabis), check-in improvement summary, BAM scores
  • Order details (e.g. prescription counts) and family support context
  • The member's primary care team member
  • Client profile (client name, sub-client, region)
This is the most heavily joined domain. It's the synthesis layer — it pulls a curated slice from almost every other domain so you can ask "did the program work?" in one place.
You can answer things like
  • What share of members hit a clinically meaningful reduction?
  • Which member characteristics line up with success?
  • Across the population, who improves, plateaus, or worsens?
Used most by: Outcomes, Clinical, Client Success

Revenue & Claims

The money side

Billing

In design

What clients owe Pelago. Potential, projected, and actual invoiced revenue.

1
The building block
A piece of client revenue (e.g. one invoice line, or one billing-model output for a client and month). "Acme Corp owed $42,000 for March, based on engagement-driven billing."
2
How it adds up
Stack pieces together to see a client's full monthly bill, total revenue across the book, or projected vs. actual revenue.
3
What it connects to
  • Nothing externally. Client name, contract terms, and the billing model output are all denormalized into the invoice record itself.
Self-contained on purpose. Invoices need to be auditable on their own, so the fields you'd usually join in (client, billing model, contract) are stamped onto each row. Fast to query, no fan-out risk.
You can answer things like
  • What did this client get invoiced for last month?
  • How does projected revenue compare to potential revenue across the book?
Used most by: Finance, RevOps, Client Success
Status: Still being designed. For current numbers, ask Finance directly.

Spectra

In design

A monthly client-level scoreboard. Eligibility, marketing, journey, and revenue rolled up together.

1
The building block
One client for one month, with totals already pulled together for you. "Acme Corp, June 2025: 8,400 eligible lives, 240 active members, 1,200 marketing sends, $42K invoiced."
2
How it adds up
Stack months together to see a client's trend over time, or stack clients together to see the entire book of business at a glance.
3
What it connects to
  • Eligibility (eligible lives counts)
  • Marketing events and web events (sends, opens, site activity)
  • Account opportunities and client billing details (deal terms, contract specifics)
  • Combined billing model and incentives (revenue and earn-back logic)
  • A date dimension (so months line up cleanly)
  • Client profile (client name, sub-client, region)
Notably not joined to Member Journey directly. Spectra is built around clients and months, not members. Member-level activity comes in pre-aggregated through eligibility and engagement metrics, not row-by-row.
Use this when you need
  • A single side-by-side view of how a client is performing month over month
  • Cross-domain numbers without stitching them together yourself
Used most by: Finance, RevOps, Leadership
Status: Shape still being scoped. Spectra is intentionally separate from Billing.

Claims

In design

Medical and pharmacy claims data from clients. Powers Pelago's ROI and savings story.

1
The building block
One medical or pharmacy claim line (one billed service, prescription, or procedure). "An ER visit for Jane, billed at $1,800, with a primary diagnosis of acute intoxication, dated February 18."
2
How it adds up
Stack claim lines together to see a member's full medical history, a client population's healthcare spend, or the savings impact Pelago is delivering vs. matched controls.
3
What it connects to
  • Just member id from Member Journey, plus one journey marker (registered date) so you can split pre- vs. post-Pelago claims
  • From Eligibility: the eligibility reference and a region-lock flag
  • Client name, sub-client, and region
Diagnoses, procedures, costs, and pharmacy details all live inside the claim record itself, not as joins. Claims is intentionally lean on joins so the file stays auditable against what the client sent.
You can answer things like
  • What is the ROI of Pelago's programs based on claims data?
Used most by: Outcomes, Actuarial, Client Success
Status: In design. ROI questions still flow through the Outcomes / Actuarial team.
